    The Work

    Integration as reconnection

    I work one-on-one with people in the aftermath of spiritual awakening, psychedelic experience, and existential crisis — the terrain that opens when something breaks through. Most people find themselves alone in it. The experiences are real. The frameworks for making sense of them are scarce. And the ordinary world offers little patience for what happened.

    What I offer is grounded in a view that most clinical models miss: integration is not primarily a private, psychological event. It is a relational process — the restoration of coherence across the nested systems you belong to. Your body. Your community. The living world. A psychedelic or spiritual experience that discloses your interconnectedness with life cannot be integrated through introspection alone. It asks to be lived into, through renewed relationship with the more-than-human world you are part of. The guiding question expands from what did this experience mean to me? to what does it mean within the broader web of life, and what does that ask of me?

    In practice, this means working with what is actually present: the disorientation after a profound opening, the grief that surfaces when familiar structures fall away, the difficulty of returning to ordinary life carrying something that changed everything. We use meditation, breathwork, somatic practice, and deliberate engagement with the natural world — not as separate techniques, but as pathways back into relational coherence. These are traditions of return, not ascent.

    I bring serious contemplative training and academic research into this space, alongside lived experience of the territory itself. Understanding the phenomenology of awakening, the neuroscience and ecology of psychedelic states, and the philosophical traditions that have mapped this terrain for centuries — these matter. Integration at depth requires both the capacity to remain present with uncertainty and a framework solid enough to hold what has been opened.

    What Changes

    The destination is surprisingly ordinary

    People who do this work over the arc of months describe a transformation that often surprises them — not into some elevated spiritual state, but into something much more liveable.

    Groundedness

    The destabilization of awakening gives way to genuine rootedness. You find that you can hold what you encountered without being consumed by it. The body becomes a home again.

    Integration

    The gap between insight and daily life narrows. What opened in you begins to express itself not as a state to return to, but as an orientation — a way of moving through the world that is quieter and more durable than any peak experience.

    Reconnection

    To other people. To the living world. To the dimensions of meaning that the experience pointed toward. The loneliness of navigating this terrain alone, without being understood, begins to lift.

    Clarity

    Your life begins to reorganize around what actually matters. Not dramatically, but steadily. The choices that once seemed difficult become more obvious. You know what you're for.

    Who This Is For

    You might recognize yourself

    You wake up at 3am with a dread that has no object. You've done the therapy, read the books, tried the meditation app — something still hasn't moved. You're suspicious of the wellness industry, bored by conventional spirituality, quietly convinced there's something underneath both. You feel the connection between what's wrong with you and what's wrong with the world, but can't quite name it.

    What these people share: sincerity — and a sense, however inchoate, that the difficulty they are in is not merely a problem to be solved, but a passage to be navigated.

    The person in the wake of a spiritual opening

    Something broke through — in a ceremony, on a retreat, in a meditation, or without any catalyst at all. Your worldview has been reorganized at a level that most people around you can't reach. You are managing the gap between what you now know and the life you're still living. You need someone who understands this territory and can help you find your footing within it, not someone who will pathologize it or spiritually inflate it.

    The person navigating a dark night of the soul

    What mystics across traditions have called the dark night is a genuine territory: the collapse of the structures that once organized meaning, identity, and direction. It can follow a peak experience, emerge from loss, or arrive without warning. It is not depression in the clinical sense, though it resembles it. It is an initiation — and like all initiations, it requires a guide who has walked through it and returned.

    The person working with psychedelic experience

    You have had a powerful experience — or you are preparing for one — and you want support that meets it with genuine understanding. Integration here is not about making the experience fit your existing life. It is about allowing the experience to reorganize your relationship with your body, your community, and the living world — the full relational field the experience opened you toward.

    The seeker who has lost the thread

    You have read the books, done the retreats, sustained a practice. Something real happened. But somewhere the thread was lost — the practice became routine, the insight didn't translate, or the path forward is no longer clear. You need someone who can meet you at the level of your actual inquiry and help you find the living edge of it again.

    The person burning out or breaking down

    You have run hard for years — achieving, building, optimizing — and something is giving out. The collapse you are experiencing may be exactly the threshold your life has been moving toward. What looks like breakdown from the outside is often the pressure of a deeper intelligence asserting itself. The question is how to meet it, not how to recover from it as quickly as possible.

    Someone living with ADHD, depression, or addiction

    These are often symptoms of a deeper relational disconnection — from the body, from community, from meaning, from the natural world. You are not broken. You are responding, often intelligently, to conditions that were never designed for a human being. I work with the whole person and the whole context, not the diagnosis.

    The person carrying ecological grief

    You feel the weight of what is being lost in the living world — not as abstraction, but as something visceral and personal. This grief is legitimate. It may also be one of the more honest responses to the time we are living in. I take it seriously as a form of ecological perception rather than a disorder to manage, and I offer space to metabolize it and find what it asks of you.

    Who I Am

    What I bring to this work

    At thirteen I was put on Ritalin. By seventeen I was eating mushrooms in the woods and falling back in love with life for the first time. By eighteen I'd gone too far — chasing visions I had no idea how to integrate, collecting insights like souvenirs from a gift shop I'd broken into. Then the dark night arrived and took everything apart.

    What came after — years of silence, Vipassana, a slow and humbling return — eventually became the work I do now. I'm not offering shortcuts. I've been on the other side of what happens when you skip the preparation and skip the integration. I know exactly what it costs. The point of psychedelics, I've come to understand, isn't transcendence. It's return. Changed, humbled, and — if you do the work — genuinely useful to the world around you.

    I offer not answers, but companionship on the walk back.

    I know this terrain from the inside. My own path has included genuine mystical openings, the long disorientation that followed them, and what I can only describe as a dark night — a period in which the structures that had organized my sense of self and purpose collapsed, and nothing obviously replaced them. What eventually emerged from that passage was not a recovery to what I had been, but a reorganization around something more real.

    Academic depth. I completed a triple major in Psychology, Philosophy, and Religious Studies at McGill University, followed by an MA in Psychedelics and Consciousness Studies at the University of Ottawa, where I developed the Psygaia framework — a systematic account of psychedelic organisms as mediators within broader ecological and cognitive systems, with direct implications for how integration ought to be understood and practiced. I intend to continue this research at the doctoral level.

    Contemplative training. My practice includes Zen Buddhist training at Enpuku-ji in Montréal, silent Vipassana retreat practice, Ashtanga yoga as a complete path, and certified instruction in conscious connected breathwork. I teach meditation, yoga, and breathwork in private practice and rehabilitation settings. I have trained in and led ceremonial contexts.

    Institutional independence. I am the founding researcher and director of Psygaia, a nonprofit dedicated to ecological and systems-based approaches to psychedelics. I co-founded Woven, which offers guided ceremonial journeys and integrative retreats. I operate outside pharmaceutical models, institutional lineages, and wellness industry incentives. I can tell you what I actually see.

    Working Together

    The deepest work happens over time

    Integration is not a task that can be completed in a single conversation. It unfolds in the continuity of a relationship — one that develops its own intelligence over months, in which I come to know your history, your patterns, and the particular shape of what you are carrying.

    Three Months
    Two sessions / month

    Long enough to establish the ground — to find what is actually present, develop a working relationship with it, and begin carrying that orientation into daily life.

    Six Months
    Two sessions / month

    Long enough for real and lasting reorganization to occur. The continuity allows for the full arc of opening and stabilization, with time for change to root itself in how you actually live.

    Ongoing
    Monthly or as needed

    For those further along who want a trusted outside perspective to return to — a reliable companion as the work continues to unfold in the context of your life.

    On Contribution

    This work is offered in the spirit of dāna — generosity according to one's means and the value received. Sessions are typically $80–200, offered on a sliding scale. There is no fixed fee. Before we begin, we speak honestly about what is possible and what is right. I make special accommodations based on genuine resonance and fit. Reach out and we will find what makes sense.

    Group retreats

    For those drawn to working in community — in nature, with others on a similar arc — I co-founded Woven, which offers guided group psychedelic journeys and integrative retreats in the BC backcountry and beyond. These are not luxury wellness retreats. They are held containers for real work, in real nature, with people who are genuinely here for it.

    Woven and 1:1 work with me are complementary — some people begin with a retreat and then continue individually; others work with me first and find their way to the group when it's right. Either path is legitimate. Learn more about upcoming retreats at Woven →

    Another way to work together

    Not everyone in this territory needs deep, frequent accompaniment. Some people — particularly those with meaningful self-awareness who have already done significant work — need something different: a trusted outside perspective they can return to periodically.

    This is a mentorship relationship. We meet once a month. You bring what is most alive: a decision you are sitting with, a pattern that keeps appearing, a direction you are trying to clarify. I offer honest perspective from the outside, and the accumulated context of someone who has known your history over time.

    It is a lighter touch. Less about going into the interior, more about orientation. Less about the depths of what isn't moving, more about seeing clearly and staying true. The relationship itself becomes a resource — a reliable outside eye from someone who understands your context and can speak freely.

    The commitment: three to twelve months, once monthly. We discuss what is right in conversation.
